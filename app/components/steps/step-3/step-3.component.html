FINANCE

Startup costs, first employees, and permits and licenses often require initial funds. Here are some resources to help finance your business.

  • Craft a Financial Plan

    An important part of your business plan is to state your current financial status and provide projections of where the money will come from and where it will go.

    Identify start-up costs and estimate expenses. Utilize this SBA template for assistance. This will be helpful if you plan to seek investment and support.

  • Identify Funding Sources

    Self-funding can be a low-cost, yet high-risk, approach to sourcing your start-up costs. Whether using personal savings, access to a retirement account, or investment from family and friends, self-funding can be affordable, limiting the fees paid on funds “borrowed” but causes you to assume all risk of loss.

    Venture capitalist investment can provide funding in return for giving equity to investors or allowing for profit sharing. Connect with GAIN for access to local investors, mentors and resources.

    Crowdfunding can help you raise small amounts of money per person from a large group of people who do not expect a return on their investment. While crowdfunding platforms may charge fees or a percent of your profits gained, it is typically a low-risk approach as a business owner.

    Obtain a small business loan from a traditional institution. Lender Match can help you find local lenders approved by the SBA and who offer SBA-guaranteed loans.

  • Evaluate Your Eligibility for Assistance & Incentive Programs

    The City of Gainesville provides several incentives to defray costs and maximize business growth.

    The City of Gainesville has adopted the Small and Service-Disabled Veteran Business Program to provide opportunities for qualified local small businesses to participate on a nondiscriminatory basis in all aspects of the City's contracting and procurement programs as well as providing other needed business services.

    The Florida Department of Economic Opportunity offers a variety of programs including loans, loan guarantees and tax credits dependent upon your industry, personal demographics or geographic location.

    The SBA partners with private investors to capitalize professionally-managed investment funds (known as "SBICs") that finance small businesses (https://www.sba.gov/sbic).
